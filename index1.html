Security Cooperation
Security cooperation authorities
Title 10 and Title 22 authorities governing partner nation capacity building, training, and equipment transfer in the SOUTHCOM AOR. Leahy Law applies to every entry on this page.
⚠
Leahy Law — no exceptions
22 USC 2378d and 10 USC 362 prohibit security assistance to any foreign security force unit that has committed gross violations of human rights without accountability. Unit-level vetting through State Department INVEST system required before any interaction. Run vetting in parallel with program planning — never in series. Serial execution adds 30–90 days and is the most common execution failure in SOUTHCOM security cooperation.
Threat & Policy
UAV / cUAS — threat, policy, acquisition
Communication architecture, offense-defense asymmetry, Safer Skies Act (FY26 NDAA), NDAA §848 hardware prohibition, and the partner nation gap.
⚠
Offense-defense asymmetry — structural, not technical
A non-state actor fields a weaponized commercial drone in hours. A defender must clear an authorized equipment list process measured in months to years. The gap compounds with every iteration cycle the adversary completes while the defender is still in testing. This is not a resource problem. It is an architectural problem.

Governing regulations
FAA Part 107Commercial UAS certification and operating rules. COA required for government operations. LAANC for airspace authorization.
DoDD 3000.09Autonomous weapons systems. Human supervisory requirements for any UAV with lethal or decision-making capability. Updated 2023.
NDAA §848 (FY20)Prohibits federal procurement of DJI, Autel, Parrot, Yuneec, and affiliated Chinese-manufactured UAS. Applies to all federally funded acquisitions. Does not cover partner nation self-procurement.
Safer Skies ActSigned Dec 18, 2025 (FY26 NDAA). Authorizes SLTT law enforcement to detect, track, disable, or destroy drones threatening critical infrastructure, major events, or correctional facilities. DHS/DOJ AEL + federal training required. Regs due ~June 2026. $250M FEMA grant.
SkyFoundry (FY26)Government-backed domestic drone manufacturing program. Congressional response to Chinese supply chain dominance.
DoDI 4650.01Spectrum management for all DoD UAS operations. Frequency authorization required. Coordination with NTIA for shared bands.
⚑
Chinese UAV / NDAA §848 — partner nation gap
SOUTHCOM partner nation security forces operate significant DJI inventories acquired outside U.S. funding streams. NDAA §848 does not reach these systems. Any joint operation or shared C2 architecture must account for the reality that friendly forces are flying hardware manufactured by the adversary — a real-time intelligence collection risk and long-term supply chain dependency that no current policy fully resolves.
Critical Infrastructure
Critical infrastructure protection
CISA 16-sector framework applied to the SOUTHCOM AOR. UAV threat vectors, SMR deployment implications, and the private sector ownership gap.
⚛ Return item — Small Modular Reactors
SMRs will be deployed in the SOUTHCOM AOR. The protection architecture does not yet exist.
SMRs will be fielded at forward operating locations, remote partner nation sites, and island chain installations in the near term. They inherit the full NRC regulatory framework — 10 CFR 73 physical security, 10 CFR 73.54 cyber security — in a smaller footprint deployed in more exposed, less defended configurations. UAV overflight, communication exploitation, and cyber-physical attack via proximate drone operations are the primary threat vectors. An SMR at a SOUTHCOM forward location is simultaneously a critical infrastructure asset and a potential high-consequence target. Acquisition planning must integrate DoE, NRC, and DoD authorities simultaneously — a coordination challenge with no existing playbook.

⚑
Private sector ownership gap
The majority of U.S. critical infrastructure is privately owned. When a UAV threat materializes against a power grid or port, decision authority sits with a utility company or port operator — not a DoD commander. Acquisition pathways available to private sector critical infrastructure owners are limited to DHS/CISA programs, Safer Skies grants (SLTT law enforcement only), and private procurement. DoD cannot assume operational control without specific authority. This gap is a primary unresolved national security vulnerability.
Governing framework
PPD-21Presidential Policy Directive 21 — Critical Infrastructure Security and Resilience. CISA as lead federal coordinator. 16 sectors with sector risk management agencies defined.
NIST CSF 2.0Cybersecurity Framework (2024). Identify-Protect-Detect-Respond-Recover. Applied to ICS/OT environments in energy, water, and nuclear sectors.
NERC CIPMandatory critical infrastructure protection standards for bulk electric system. Enforceable with financial penalties. Physical and cyber perimeter protection.
10 CFR 73NRC physical security for nuclear facilities. Background investigations, armed guards, access control. SMR deployments require NRC site security plan approval.
10 CFR 73.54NRC cyber security. Protects digital systems associated with safety, security, and emergency preparedness. Applies to SMRs including forward-deployed configurations.
DHS SAFETY ActLiability protection for DHS-designated anti-terrorism technologies. Relevant for cUAS vendors in U.S. CI protection. Does not apply outside U.S. territory.

Spectrum Management
Spectrum — the silent constraint
The electromagnetic spectrum is finite, contested, and governed by overlapping authorities moving at incompatible speeds. In the SOUTHCOM AOR, partner nation spectrum governance ranges from weak to nonexistent. The adversary has no coordination requirements.
⚑
18–24 month certification lag — the procurement tail
Testing and certification for spectrum-touching systems (UAV, cUAS, tactical comms) takes 18–24 months. The technology that clears the process is already a generation behind the threat. This is the single largest speed-killer in cUAS acquisition and directly undermines the Safer Skies Act's operational intent.
Technical layer
UAVs, cUAS, 5G, tactical radios, GPS, and commercial wireless compete for overlapping bands with no real-time arbitration in contested environments. A cUAS jammer may simultaneously disable friendly comms, GPS, or partner nation civilian infrastructure on adjacent frequencies.
Regulatory layer
FCC, NTIA, and DoD each govern portions of spectrum at different timelines. A new DoD system requires NTIA frequency authorization. Commercial systems require FCC. Partner nation operations require bilateral coordination agreements — which often do not exist in the SOUTHCOM AOR.
Operational layer
The adversary has no coordination requirements. Commercial drones operate on unlicensed Part 15 bands — promiscuous and impossible to pre-authorize against. State-actor UAVs use frequency-agile systems designed to defeat static jamming. The defender protects a fixed plan. The attacker shifts at will.
Governing framework
NTIA ManualManual of Regulations and Procedures for Federal Radio Frequency Management. Federal spectrum allocation and use. Authorization process: 6–18 months typical.
FCC Part 15Unlicensed operations. Most commercial drone C2 (900MHz, 2.4GHz, 5.8GHz ISM) operates here — no license, no coordination, no enforcement against interference.
DoDI 4650.01DoD spectrum management policy. Frequency authorization for all DoD transmitting systems. Theater spectrum management through SOUTHCOM J6.
47 USC 333Prohibits willful interference with radio communications. cUAS jamming by non-authorized entities may violate this statute. Federal agencies have waivers; SLTT under Safer Skies with authorized equipment only.
Safer Skies AELAuthorized equipment list under development (DHS/DOJ regs due ~June 2026). Adds a certification layer on top of NTIA/FCC — compounding the procurement tail.
